Competing Ways of Knowing Water

The management of the Panama Canal watershed is articulated through a technical language of forecasts, basin modeling, and infrastructure planning. In official discourse, water is treated as a quantifiable resource that can be redistributed across time through reservoirs, dams, and hydrological projections.

43rd Annual Patterson Lecture: Ricaurte Vásquez Morales, Panama Canal Administrator

Vasquez delivered the 43rd Annual William A. Patterson Transportation Lecture, held May 7, 2025, at the Walter Athletics Center Auditorium. Hosted by the Northwestern University Transportation Center (NUTC), the lecture is named for William A. “Pat” Patterson, a central figure in the US air transport industry for more than four decades who served as president and chairman of United Airlines from 1934 until his retirement in 1966. He was a life trustee of Northwestern University and was instrumental in the establishment and strategic leadership of the NUTC.

This epistemology assumes that territory can be rendered legible through measurement: rainfall becomes data, rivers become supply, and rural basins become operational extensions of the Canal system.

However, this mode of knowledge does not exist in isolation. It is continuously confronted by other forms of knowing territory—forms that do not separate water from land, or land from memory, or memory from the conditions of survival.

Across the documentary materials and interviews assembled in this project, Digna appears within this tension. Her account does not translate easily into the language of planning or policy. Instead, it registers as lived anticipation: a relationship to land shaped by the possibility of displacement that precedes its formal announcement.

What emerges is not a single narrative of the watershed, but a field of competing epistemologies. One organizes water through future capacity and infrastructure design. The other experiences water through uncertainty, continuity, and the fragility of dwelling within a territory that is being reclassified in advance of intervention.

The watershed, in this sense, is not only a hydrological unit. It is a site where different ways of knowing territory are placed into sustained confrontation without resolution.
